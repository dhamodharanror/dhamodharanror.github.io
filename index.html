I build systems. I ship things.

I can design multi-tenant systems that actually scale

CultureMonkey runs every customer in its own isolated PostgreSQL schema. Subdomain middleware switches context per request. Background jobs carry tenant context automatically — no manual switching, no cross-tenant exposure. Survey lifecycle, participant flows, autosave, and submission all operate cleanly within this model.

I designed the architecture that keeps it this way as the platform grew, and I've kept it from accumulating the kind of shortcuts that eventually break multi-tenant systems.

I can integrate anything

If there's an API, I'll connect to it cleanly. Things I've shipped:

  • Slack bot — OAuth install, interactive survey delivery, response capture
  • Microsoft Teams — adaptive card surveys, tenant-level bot install, proactive messaging
  • HRIS ecosystem — BambooHR, Workday, Keka, Darwinbox, ZohoPeople, FreshService and more. Provider factory pattern. First-sync, incremental sync, employee hierarchy. Adding a new connector doesn't touch existing ones.
  • SAML SSO — SP/IdP-initiated with Google and custom enterprise IdPs, Microsoft OAuth, TOTP 2FA, CanCanCan RBAC, all tenant-aware
  • WhatsApp, SMS (Twilio), email — notification dispatch with retry and failure handling

I have a thing for performance

When reports were slow, I didn't tune queries in circles — I redesigned the pipeline.

Built a dual-pipeline architecture: Elasticsearch handles real-time search and segment filtering. ClickHouse ingests on a 5-minute schedule for heavy aggregations — response trends, benchmark comparisons, cross-account analytics. Report query times dropped significantly. The two systems serve different needs and don't get in each other's way.

I deliver — consistently

Seven years at the same product company. Not because I couldn't leave — because there was always more worth building.

I believe in shipping over perfecting. When a problem comes in, I'll usually offer a few solutions with different costs and tradeoffs. Sometimes the right answer is a careful redesign. Sometimes it's a targeted fix that ships today. I've learned to tell the difference.

I can lead a team without becoming a bottleneck

I care about code review that teaches, not just approves. About hiring people I'd want to learn from. About technical direction that's clear enough that the team can make good decisions without checking with me first.

Energy and clarity are real forces in an engineering team. I've seen what happens when both are present, and I try to protect them.

And I have strong opinions, weakly held

  • On multi-tenancy: schema-per-tenant is more expensive upfront and worth it.
  • On background jobs: if the job doesn't know its tenant context automatically, the architecture is wrong.
  • On integrations: a provider factory that's boring to extend is better than a clever one that's fragile.
  • On Rails: it's not legacy. It's productive. Stop apologising for it.
  • On tech decisions: the best architecture is the one your team can reason about at 2am when something breaks.
